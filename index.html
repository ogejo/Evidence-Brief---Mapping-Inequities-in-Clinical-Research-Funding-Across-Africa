Evidence Brief

Mapping Inequities in Clinical Research Funding Across Africa, 2007–2023.

A Data-Driven Case for Transforming How the World Invests in African Health Research

Charles Ogejo  ·  March 2026

This document contains interactive visualisations that are best accessed on a large screen, preferably in landscape orientation.

This brief synthesises findings from a comprehensive analysis of 305,334 funding records from the Clinical Trials Community - Africa (CTC Africa)1 database and the Global Burden of Disease (GBD) 20232 disease burden estimates across all 54 African countries. It presents the evidence, explains what it means for the 1.4 billion people living on the continent, and outlines a path from structural inequity to research sovereignty.

All required R packages were loaded upfront. Key packages include tidyverse for data wrangling, circlize for chord diagrams, igraph/ggraph for network analysis, and sf/rnaturalearth for spatial data.

Step 1.2-1.4 - Importing, Inspecting & Combining funding data from CTC Africa
CTC Africa direct and indirect funding records were imported, filtered to 54 African countries, and restricted to 2007-2023. Combined dataset: 305,334 records across 39 recipient countries.
# Loading direct and indirect funding streams
ctc_direct   <- read_csv("data/ctc_direct.csv")
ctc_indirect <- read_csv("data/ctc_indirect.csv")

# Combining, filtering to Africa, applying restriction to study window
ctc_combined <- bind_rows(
  ctc_direct  %>% mutate(funding_stream = "Direct"),
  ctc_indirect %>% mutate(funding_stream = "Indirect")
) %>%
  mutate(year_num = as.numeric(str_extract(year, "\\d{4}"))) %>%
  filter(recipient_country %in% african_countries_ctc,
         year_num >= 2007, year_num <= 2023)
Step 1.5 - Handling Multi-Country Grants
Grants recorded against multiple recipient countries were split equally to prevent double-counting of funding amounts. From the imported, combined dataset, there were no Multi-Country Grants.
# Dividing shared grants equally across recipient countries
ctc_split <- ctc_combined %>%
  separate_rows(recipient_country, sep = ",|;\\s*") %>%
  group_by(across(-c(recipient_country, amount_usd))) %>%
  mutate(n_countries = n(),
         amount_split = amount_usd / n_countries) %>%
  ungroup()
Steps 1.6-1.7 - Parsing & Filtering GBD DALY Data
The GBD 2023 file contained 705 stacked location sections. A custom parser extracted disease-level DALY estimates for each African country, yielding 2021 all-age DALY counts as the burden denominator.
# Customizing parser for stacked GBD location format
gbd_raw     <- read_csv("data/gbd_raw.csv", col_names = FALSE)
header_rows <- which(str_detect(gbd_raw[[1]], "All-age DALY counts"))

# Extracting point estimate (stripping uncertainty intervals)
extract_point_estimate <- function(x) {
  as.numeric(str_remove_all(str_trim(str_extract(x, "^[^\\n(]+")), ",")))
}

# Parsing all sections and filtering to 54 African countries
gbd_africa <- map2_dfr(header_rows, end_rows, parse_gbd_section) %>%
  filter(location %in% african_countries_gbd)

Steps 2.3-2.5 - Aggregating & Merging Funding with Disease Burden
In this step, funding is aggregated to country–disease level. DALYs are similarly aggregated using 2021 estimates. A full outer join produces 1,146 country–disease pairs with measurable burden.
# Aggregate funding to country-disease level
funding_agg <- ctc_mapped %>%
  filter(!is.na(standard_category),
         standard_category != "Cross-cutting R&D") %>%
  group_by(country, standard_category) %>%
  summarise(total_funding = sum(amount_split, na.rm = TRUE), .groups = "drop")

# Full outer join: every country-disease pair with any burden
funding_burden <- funding_agg %>%
  full_join(daly_agg, by = c("country" = "location", "standard_category")) %>%
  mutate(total_funding = replace_na(total_funding, 0))
Steps 2.6-2.7 - Computing Funding per DALY & Equity Ratio
The primary analytical metric - funding per DALY - is calculated for each country–disease pair. An equity ratio is derived by normalising against the African continental median ($7.39/DALY). Pairs are classified into four equity categories.
# Primary metric: funding per DALY
equity_metrics <- funding_burden %>%
  filter(!is.na(total_dalys), total_dalys > 0) %>%
  mutate(funding_per_daly = total_funding / total_dalys)

# African continental median (funded pairs only)
african_median_fpd <- median(
  equity_metrics$funding_per_daly[equity_metrics$funding_per_daly > 0]
) # → $7.39/DALY

# Equity categories
equity_metrics <- equity_metrics %>%
  mutate(equity_ratio = funding_per_daly / african_median_fpd,
    equity_category = case_when(
      funding_per_daly == 0       ~ "Zero Funding",
      equity_ratio < 0.5          ~ "Far Below Continental Median",
      equity_ratio <= 1.5        ~ "Near Continental Median",
      TRUE                         ~ "Above Continental Median"
    ))
Step 2.8 - Building Country-Level Feature Table
Five features were computed per country for use as clustering inputs: total funding per DALY, disease breadth (number of areas funded), funder breadth, top-funder concentration share, and total DALYs.
# Aggregating to country level - 5 clustering features
country_features <- equity_metrics %>%
  group_by(country) %>%
  summarise(
    total_funding_all        = sum(total_funding),
    funding_per_daly_country = total_funding_all / sum(total_dalys),
    disease_breadth          = n_distinct(standard_category[total_funding > 0])
  ) %>%
  left_join(funder_metrics, by = "country")
# funder_metrics adds: funder_breadth, top_funder_share

Part I: The Crisis

The Numbers That Should Keep Us Awake

Africa carries roughly a quarter of the global disease burden3. Every year, hundreds of millions of disability-adjusted life years (DALYs) - each one representing a year of healthy life destroyed by disease, disability, or premature death - accumulate across the continent. Against that staggering human cost, Africa receives less than 2% of global clinical research funding4,5.

This analysis transforms that abstract disparity into a precise, granular measurement. By combining 17 years of CTC Africa funding data1 with the Global Burden of Disease 2023 estimates2, we calculated a single metric - funding per DALY- for every country-disease combination on the continent. The results are not merely disappointing. They reveal a system that is structurally configured to exclude.

What $7.39 Per DALY Actually Means

The African continental median, as computed in this analysis, is $7.39 per DALY. The high-income benchmark, estimated from WHO and G-FINDER data5, sits between $50 and $100 per DALY. That is a 10–15X gap.

To understand what this means in practice, when a child develops neonatal sepsis in Lagos, the clinical protocol she receives was almost certainly developed from evidence generated in high-income settings. This means different pathogens, different resistance patterns, different nutritional baselines, and different healthcare delivery contexts. The drugs were dosed for populations that do not reflect her physiology. The diagnostics were calibrated for disease presentations she may not share. Her doctor is working without locally generated evidence, because no one funded the research to produce it.

Now consider that $7.39 is the median. Meaning half the continent falls below it. The Underserved Periphery countries operate at $0.49 per DALY. That is 153 times below the high-income benchmark. And 15 countries, home to roughly 280 million people, receive nothing at all.

80.2% of Country-Disease Pairs Receive Zero Funding

This is the finding that reframes the entire conversation. Out of 1,146 measurable country-disease pairs across Africa - combinations where a specific disease causes quantifiable burden in a specific country - 919 receive zero clinical research funding. Not inadequate funding. Not below-average funding. Nothing.

These are not obscure, rare conditions in tiny populations. These are malaria in the DRC (5.2 million DALYs, zero funding). Neonatal disorders in Nigeria (23 million DALYs, zero funding). Lower respiratory infections across half the continent - diseases that fill paediatric wards every rainy season - with no one funding the clinical trials that might improve treatment protocols for African children.

Each unfunded DALY is not an accounting line. It is a year of healthy life destroyed by disability, suffering, or death, in a context where no one is generating the evidence that might prevent it.

Concentration That Would Trigger Antitrust Action

The Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) for research funding across African countries, as computed in this analysis, is 2,972. Under the 2010 U.S. Department of Justice & Federal Trade Commission Horizontal Merger Guidelines, an HHI above 2,500 signals a highly concentrated market6. The 2023 revision lowered the presumption threshold further to 1,8007. The research funding landscape across Africa exceeds both.

South Africa alone absorbs more than 50% of all continental funding. The Lorenz concentration curve bends at the very first country. Ten countries capture 89.2%. The remaining 44 countries, representing hundreds of millions of people, share the remaining 10.8%.

This is not a funding gap. This is a funding architecture that systematically directs resources to a small number of established hubs while rendering the rest of the continent invisible.

Part II: The Structure

Four Africas, Not One

The clustering analysis, (K-means with k=3 for funded countries plus a fourth tier for structurally excluded nations), reveals that the 54 African countries do not exist on a spectrum. They fall into four qualitatively different typologies, each requiring fundamentally different policy responses.

Typology 1: Diversified Research Ecosystems (7 countries)

South Africa, Kenya, Nigeria, Ghana, Uganda, Ethiopia, and Tanzania.

These countries have built genuine research ecosystems. Their median funding per DALY is $12.40. They cover a median of 11 disease areas, attract a median of 29 unique funders, and their dependency on any single funder is relatively low at 25%. Together, they absorb approximately 87% of continental funding.

But even these seven best-resourced nations on the continent operate at 8-17% of high-income benchmarks. South Africa's $121 per DALY looks impressive until you compare it to the approximately $142 per person the United States invests through NIH alone8. The Diversified countries are not adequately funded, but rather the least inadequately funded.

The best-funded country on the continent still invests less per unit of disease burden than the least-funded research programme in most European nations.

Typology 2: Emerging Research Recipients (19 countries)

Benin, Botswana, Burkina Faso, Cameroon, Congo, Côte d’Ivoire, DRC, Gabon, Gambia, Guinea, Madagascar, Malawi, Mali, Mozambique, Rwanda, Senegal, Sudan, Zambia, Zimbabwe.

These are transition states. Their median funding per DALY is $4.54, they cover roughly 7 disease areas, and they attract around 12 funders. Their single-funder concentration of 36% sits at the fragility threshold, meaning the countries have just enough enough diversity to function, not enough to withstand a major withdrawal.

The 19 Emerging countries collectively receive about 11% of continental funding. They represent the pool from which the next generation of Diversified ecosystems might emerge, or without sustained investment, the pool that slides backward.

Typology 3: Underserved Periphery (13 countries)

Angola, Chad, Egypt, Tunisia, Niger, Sierra Leone, Burundi, Central African Republic, Eswatini, Guinea-Bissau, Liberia, Namibia, and Togo.

This is where the data becomes most damning. These 13 countries, with a combined population of over 300 million, receive just 1.6% of continental research funding. Their median funding per DALY is $0.49.

But the most alarming metric is not the funding level, but the concentration. A median of 83% of each country's entire research funding comes from a single source. They cover only 2 disease areas, with only 2 funders. For the countries in this typology, when a funder's strategy shifts as it happens routinely, an entire country's research capacity can evaporate overnight.

Typology 4: Structurally Excluded (15 countries)

Algeria, Libya, Morocco, Somalia, South Sudan, Eritrea, Lesotho, Mauritania, Cabo Verde, Comoros, Djibouti, Equatorial Guinea, Sao Tome and Principe, Seychelles, and Mauritius.

Across 17 years of data, these 15 countries have no documented clinical research funding on the CTC Africa platform. They are not underfunded. They are invisible. Absent from the databases, absent from the networks, absent from the conversation.

Some of these nations carry enormous disease burdens. Somalia and South Sudan face among the highest mortality rates on the continent. Others, like Algeria and Morocco, have healthcare systems capable of absorbing research investment but are excluded from the predominantly sub-Saharan architecture of international health research funding. The exclusion is structural, not incidental.

What the Typologies Mean for Policy

The critical insight is that a single policy cannot address four qualitatively different situations. Telling the Diversified countries to "expand disease coverage" and telling the Excluded countries the same thing is meaningless because the latter have no research infrastructure to expand. The typology framework provides the diagnostic precision that policy has lacked: each cluster requires a fundamentally different intervention logic, timeline, and investment profile.

Part III: The Network

Who Funds Whom, and What It Reveals

Clustering tells us what countries look like. Network analysis tells us why they look that way. By constructing a bipartite graph connecting every funder to every country they support - weighted by funding volume - we mapped the structural architecture of African research funding.

An Extremely Sparse Network

The network, as constructed in this analysis, contains 142 nodes (103 funders and 39 funded countries) linked by 491 edges (funding relationships). The density is 0.049. This means that only 4.9% of all possible funder-country connections actually exist. The remaining 95% are gaps.

For comparison, research on resilient collaboration networks suggests that functionally redundant networks, where countries would have multiple alternative pathways to access investment, typically exhibit densities in the range of 0.15-0.25. At 0.049, the African research funding landscape is three to five times sparser than what structural resilience would require.

Gatekeepers and the South Africa Hub Effect

EDCTP leads in breadth, reaching 33 countries - the widest footprint of any funder on the continent. FIND reaches 28, WHO/TDR reaches 25. But the US NIH, despite being the single largest investor at $735 million, reaches only 19 countries. The Gates Foundation invests $673 million across just 18 countries.

This creates a structural paradox: the funders with the most money have the narrowest reach, while the funders with the broadest reach have less money to distribute. High-value, narrow-reach funding concentrates in established hubs. Broad-reach, lower-value funding spreads thin.

South Africa sits at the apex with 52 funder connections and $1.9 billion in total investment. It operates as the continental gateway. Funders entering Africa often establish partnerships with South African institutions first, and some never move beyond them. Ghana (36 connections), Kenya (31), and Uganda (29) form a second tier. Below that, connectivity drops steeply.

What Sparse Connectivity Means in Practice

Consider a researcher at the University of N'Djamena in Chad. The Underserved Periphery averages 2 funder connections. If those two funders shift priorities, as it has been happening across multiple agencies during COVID-19 reprogramming and beyond, that researcher has no alternative pathway. There is no fallback application, no secondary funder, no consortium to absorb the shock. The research programme simply ends.

Now consider a researcher at the University of Cape Town. With 52 funder connections, the loss of any single one is likely absorbed by the remaining 51. South Africa's research ecosystem has structural redundancy. Chad's does not. The network does not just reflect inequality, but reproduces it through preferential attachment, where connected nodes attract more connections while isolated nodes stay isolated.

Research funding operates as an exclusive club. Once connected, a country attracts more connections. Once excluded, a country remains excluded. The network perpetuates what it inherits.

Six Communities, Six Funding Orbits

Louvain community detection identified 6 distinct communities within the network (modularity = 0.308), revealing that funders and countries cluster into semi-independent orbits. European institutional funders (EDCTP, EC) form one community centred on francophone and anglophone West Africa. US agencies (NIH, CDC) form another centred on Southern and East Africa. Philanthropies (Gates, Wellcome) form a third, overlapping with US agencies but extending into different disease areas.

The practical consequence is that a country's placement within a funding community determines which diseases get studied, which methodologies dominate, and whose priorities shape the research agenda. Countries at the intersection of multiple communities such as South Africa, Kenya, and Ghana benefit from diverse perspectives. Countries in the periphery receive whatever one community's priorities dictate.

Nigeria: The World's Largest Blind Spot

Nigeria accounts for 6 of the top 10 priority gaps. A nation of 220 million people, the most populous in Africa, the seventh most populous on earth, has 23 million DALYs of neonatal disease with zero clinical research investment. Not low investment. Zero.

To make this concrete: approximately 250,000 Nigerian newborns die each year from neonatal conditions such as sepsis, birth asphyxia, prematurity complications. These are complications for which improved interventions exist but have never been tested in the Nigerian context. The treatment protocols used in Nigerian Teaching Hospitals are based on evidence from trials in Europe and North America. None of the drug dosages, the diagnostic cut-offs, or the management algorithms were generated from data on Nigerian neonates.

This is not because neonatal research is impossible in Nigeria. It is because the funding architecture has never directed resources there. Nigeria's total research funding across all diseases and all years is $29.8 million - roughly what a single mid-sized clinical trial costs in a high-income country.

The Equity Ratio: Measuring Distance from Fairness

We computed equity ratios by dividing each country-disease pair's funding per DALY by the continental median. Nigeria's malaria funding, for instance, has an equity ratio of 0.04. This means it receives 4% of what the already severely inadequate African median would suggest. Only 8.2% of all country-disease pairs fall above the continental median. The distribution is not skewed. It is collapsed at zero.

The equity category breakdown tells the story starkly: 80.2% receive zero funding, 7.2% fall far below the continental median, 4.5% are near the median, and 8.2% are above. Even the "above median" category is misleading, because the median itself is $7.39, a fraction of global benchmarks that range between $50–$100. Being "above the African median" in this case can be likened to being the tallest person in a room where everyone is sitting down.

Part V: Why This Happens

The Self-Reinforcing Architecture of Exclusion

The inequities documented here are not the result of malice or even negligence. They are the predictable output of a funding system designed around three assumptions, each of which the data now disproves.

Assumption 1: "Fund the Best Science"

Competitive funding rewards existing capacity. Countries with established institutions, experienced investigators, and track records of successful grants attract more funding. Countries without these, because they were never funded in the first place, cannot even compete. The result is self-reinforcing: 87% of continental funding flows to 7 countries, because those are the countries with the capacity to win grants, and they have the capacity because they received funding historically.

This is not a meritocratic system identifying excellence. It is a system that mistakes the consequences of prior investment for intrinsic capability.

Assumption 2: "Research Transfers Across Borders"

The assumption that a clinical trial conducted in South Africa generates knowledge applicable in Niger is increasingly understood to be flawed. Pharmacogenomics research has demonstrated that African populations harbour the greatest genetic diversity on earth - drug metabolism, disease susceptibility, and treatment response vary more within Africa than between Africa and the rest of the world9,10.

A comprehensive review of cytochrome P450 polymorphisms across African populations found greater diversity in CYP distribution in Africa than in any other continental population, and identified multiple drug-metabolising enzyme alleles that appear unique to African populations10,11. Malaria parasites in West Africa carry different resistance profiles than those in East Africa. Neonatal pathogens in Lagos differ from those in Nairobi. Context-specific research is not a luxury, but a scientific necessity.

Assumption 3: "Capacity Must Precede Funding"

This is the catch-22 situation that traps the Underserved Periphery and the Structurally Excluded countries. Funders require institutional capacity before disbursing grants. But institutional capacity requires funding to build. The 15 countries with zero funding over 17 years are not excluded because they lack potential. They are excluded because the system requires them to demonstrate something they can only develop with the resources it withholds.

The network analysis confirms the mechanism: preferential attachment dynamics mean that connected countries attract more connections while disconnected countries remain disconnected. Without deliberate intervention, the system will continue to concentrate resources where they already exist.

Part VI: What Must Change

Five Evidence-Based Interventions

This analysis did not merely diagnose the clinical reasearch funding problem on the continent. The typology framework, the network structure, and the priority gap identification together provide the specificity that has lacked. Each intervention suggested below is matched to the evidence that justifies it.

1. Burden-Proportional Allocation Mandates

The extreme concentration of research funding, reflected in an HHI of 2,972 and 89.2% of all investment flowing to just ten countries, demonstrates conclusively that market-driven allocation has failed to distribute resources in line with need. This makes burden-proportional allocation mandates an essential corrective measure.

Major funders should commit to allocating a defined share of their portfolios proportional to disease burden rather than historical relationships.To sustain accountability, the funders should establish a public equity dashboard, updated annually in tracking HHI, concentration curves, and equity ratios by funder, since transparent accountability forces correction in ways that private advocacy alone cannot.

2. Diversify the Funder Base for Peripheral Countries

The finding that 83% of Underserved Periphery countries depend on a single funder for the entirety of their research investment reveals a degree of existential fragility that demands deliberate diversification of the funder base.

Co-funding mandates should require that no country's research investment depends on fewer than three sources, emphasizing public funding by the respective African governments, African Union bodies (particularly the Africa CDC), regional economic communities, and South-South partnerships into the funding landscape, alongside Northern partners.

A funder consortium with explicit portfolio diversification targets, aiming for less than 60% concentration from any single source, would provide the structural mechanism to move peripheral countries from dependency on individual benefactors toward resilient, multi-source funding architectures.

3. Ring-Fenced Investment in Neonatal and NTD Research

The fact that the top five priority gaps identified by this analysis are all in neonatal disorders - accounting for over 43 million DALYs with zero research funding across Nigeria, Ethiopia, the Democratic Republic of the Congo, Mali, and Tanzania - exposes a structural blind spot in the current funding architecture that its infectious-disease orientation systematically reinforces.

Addressing this imbalance requires not just incremental adjustment but also deliberate correction through dedicated funding streams for systematically excluded disease categories. This includes, among others, neonatal, maternal, and neglected tropical disease research.

Interventions that would begin to close this egregious mismatch between burden and investment on the continent could include a targeted Neonatal Research Initiative directed at the five countries that appear most frequently in the top twenty priority gaps, and funding specifically context-adapted clinical trials.

4. Inclusion Pathways for Structurally Excluded Countries

The existence of fifteen countries that are home to 280 million people with zero documented research funding over the seventeen years, represents a systemic exclusion that cannot be remedied by conventional competitive grant mechanisms. This is because these countries lack the institutional base to absorb such funding in the first place.

The intervention must therefore be infrastructure-first: sequencing investment in laboratories, training, regulatory frameworks, and data systems over three to five years before research grants follow, building the absorptive capacity that makes meaningful participation possible.

Critically, this approach need not be designed from scratch. The hub-and-spoke funding model, employed by the Science for Africa Foundation and its partners in initiatives such as DELTAS Africa and the Africa Pandemic Sciences Collaborative, has already demonstrated how anchor institutions in research-strong countries can be structurally linked to those in underesourced contexts. The funding model guarantees the channelling of mentorship, resources, and institutional support outward from established hubs to developing spokes.

Embedding structurally excluded countries within such existing regional research consortia makes network integration a condition of funding rather than an optional add-on, so that investment in Kenya requires partnership with Somalia and funding to South Africa extends to Lesotho. This approach would leverage proven architecture to bring the continent's most marginalised research systems into the fold.

Closing

The Question We Must Answer

This analysis mapped the architecture of inequity in clinical research funding across Africa with granular precision. The numbers are severe: 80.2% of country-disease pairs unfunded, an HHI of 2,972, 15 countries with zero investment, and neonatal conditions representing the largest burden on the continent receiving nothing.

But numbers, however stark, are abstractions. Behind each one is a ward full of premature infants in Kano, managed with protocols that were never validated for their bodies. A laboratory in N'Djamena that closed because its sole funder shifted priorities. A researcher in Mogadishu who left for Stockholm because no one would fund her work on drug-resistant tuberculosis in the Horn of Africa.

The question this evidence compels is not whether Africa can afford clinical research. It is whether the world can afford to continue without it.

Pandemics do not respect funding typologies. Drug resistance does not stop at borders. Climate-driven disease expansion does not wait for capacity to be built. Africa's 1.4 billion people are not a peripheral concern. They are the demographic future of the planet. By 2050, one in four humans will be African. The research infrastructure built or not built today will determine the health outcomes of two billion people.

African Governments must commit to intentionally drive investments into contextual clinical research. Global funders must commit to intentional redistribution of resources toward the most underserved contexts. The global health community must commit to a new paradigm of equity that recognizes that the health of the planet's future majority cannot be an afterthought in the research agenda of today.
